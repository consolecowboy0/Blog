# Dustin Landers - Voice & Style Guide

Based on detailed analysis of "Why F1 Overtaking Broke" and "Introducing Myself." Every pattern below is documented with direct quotes.

---

## 1. Hyphens vs. Em Dashes (CRITICAL)

Dustin **never** uses em dashes. He uses **plain hyphens** (`-`) as casual dashes, with no spaces before or after in most cases. This is a core visual signature of his writing.

**Examples:**
- `"the mathematical balance that made wheel-to-wheel racing possible- and the regulation change that shattered it."` (hyphen directly abutting the word before, space after)
- `"there's real controversy behind the clickbait- and when I started poking at it with math"` (same pattern: word-hyphen-space)
- `"what I found- well- prettty interesting I thought"` (double interruption, hyphens as casual breath marks)
- `"after all that fury- the turbulent air, the mechanical screaming- they ended up separated"` (parenthetical aside bounded by hyphens)
- `"a regulatory thermostat- adjusting aero rules, tweaking car weights"` (appositional dash)
- `"Together- and this is the part that keeps me up at night- they dismantled the overtaking equation"` (interjection set off by hyphens)

**Pattern:** The hyphen-as-dash always has no space before and one space after, EXCEPT when used as a closing parenthetical dash (e.g., `"the mechanical screaming- they"`) where the same no-space-before, space-after pattern holds.

**NEVER use:** `--`, `---`, or the Unicode em dash character.

## 2. Sentence-Level Patterns

### 2a. Fragments as Emphasis
Dustin frequently deploys sentence fragments, especially short punchy ones after a longer sentence. They land like verbal punctuation marks.

- `"That's it. That's the whole universe for right now."`
- `"The balance is real. It's measurable. And the 2026 regulations just blew it to pieces."`
- `"Just- all of it. Threw the whole kitchen at this thing."`
- `"Just- poof. Gone."`
- `"Cool. Great. Exactly what everyone wanted. (Nobody wanted this.)"`
- `"That's not racing. That's a spreadsheet."`

**Pattern:** Series of 2-4 very short declarative sentences in a row, often beginning with "That's" or a single word. Used to nail a point home after a complex explanation.

### 2b. Conversational Asides and Direct Address
He breaks the fourth wall constantly, speaking directly to the reader as if mid-conversation.

- `"Right? Like just sit with that for a second."`
- `"I mean come on."`
- `"And honestly? They were reallyyy good at it."`
- `"Which- for anyone who has messed around in racing video games can tell you thats pretty intuitive."`
- `"not to be dramatic- but those are fundamentally different sports."`
- `"I just said it so you'd keep reading."`
- `"So I'm here to share."`

**Pattern:** These asides feel like the author turning to the camera in a documentary. They often begin with "Right?", "Like", "I mean", "And honestly?", or "Which-". They use intentionally casual grammar.

### 2c. Deliberate Misspellings / Stretched Words
Used sparingly for tonal emphasis, conveying a spoken quality:

- `"prettty interesting I thought"`
- `"reallyyy good at it"`

**Pattern:** Stretching a letter (usually a consonant or vowel in the middle of the word) to mimic how the word would sound if spoken emphatically. Very rare - maybe once or twice per article. Never more.

### 2d. Rhetorical Setups with Dramatic Reveals
He builds a technical observation, then delivers a one-line payoff:

- `"38ms. That's the gap between two Formula 1 cars after six hundred meters of straight-line battle..."` (number first, then the explanation)
- `"One ratio outperformed them all."`
- `"The geometry just... tells you."`

**Pattern:** Often a short sentence followed by a longer one that unpacks it, or a long buildup followed by a terse conclusion.

### 2e. The "That is the [X]" Construction
Used to label and frame concepts formally after explaining them informally:

- `"That is the first advantage: **speed**, born from aerodynamics."`
- `"That is the second advantage: **line**, born from geometry."`

**Pattern:** Parallel structure with bold key terms. This labeling device turns casual explanation into something that feels like a textbook entry, creating a satisfying formal/informal tension.

## 3. Paragraph Structure and Length

### Short Paragraphs (1-2 sentences)
Used frequently for emphasis, transitions, and punchlines:

- `"The balance is real. It's measurable. And the 2026 regulations just blew it to pieces."` (standalone paragraph)
- `"And then 2026 threw it all away."` (one-sentence paragraph as section closer)
- `"The diagnosis is clear. The math also points toward a cure."` (transition paragraph)

### Medium Paragraphs (3-5 sentences)
The workhorse. Technical explanations live here. Each sentence typically adds one new fact or observation:

- The paragraph beginning `"Without DRS, drafting gave roughly 4 km/h..."` builds from one fact (no DRS speed) to next (with DRS speed) to conclusion (near-tripling).

### Long Paragraphs (6+ sentences)
Reserved for the core analytical passages where a chain of reasoning must be followed without interruption. Even these are broken by short asides:

- The `"I pulled four full seasons..."` paragraph is the longest, covering methodology, and it still includes the aside `"with the help of AI of course. Who also made this plot below, with a title I do not approve of."`

**Key rule:** Long paragraphs are always followed by short ones. The rhythm is long-short-long-short, like breathing.

## 4. Section Structure and Header Style

### Header Patterns
Headers are short, punchy, and often irreverent or dramatic:

- `"The Worst Cars Ever Made"` (provocative, immediately undercut in the text)
- `"Two Advantages, One Corner"` (structural, X-and-Y format)
- `"The Theorem Nobody Expected, Wanted, or Cares About"` (comic tricolon)
- `"One Number to Rule Them All"` (pop culture reference, Lord of the Rings)
- `"The Machine That Kept the Balance"` (metaphorical)
- `"What 2026 Broke"` (blunt, active)
- `"Two Kinds of Passing"` (structural)
- `"The Fix"` (terse, confident)

**Pattern:** Markdown `##` headers. No title case beyond standard capitalization. Often use a dramatic or slightly humorous framing. Never boring or purely descriptive (never "Analysis of Overtaking Data"). The header promises something interesting.

### Section Dividers
Horizontal rules (`---`) are used between every major section. Each section is a self-contained movement.

### Section Internal Structure
Each section follows a rough pattern:
1. **Hook** - An arresting opening sentence or short paragraph
2. **Context** - Set up the situation or concept
3. **Technical core** - The actual data/analysis, interwoven with accessible analogies
4. **Punchline** - A short, emphatic closing that often bridges to the next section

## 5. How Technical Content Is Introduced and Explained

### The Casual-to-Precise Pipeline
Technical concepts are introduced casually, then made precise:

- `"a piece of mathematics so elementary it lives in the first month of calculus"` (the intermediate value theorem, named after being explained intuitively)
- `"like Venetian blinds"` (active aerodynamics, analogy before jargon)
- `"MGU-K's (Motor Generator Unit-Kinetic, the car's electric motor)"` (acronym then parenthetical plain-English definition)

### Numbers Are Always Grounded
Raw numbers are immediately contextualized:

- `"38ms... less time than a single frame of cinema film sits on the screen"`
- `"Eighteen kilometers per hour of free speed in the model; in practice, at real racing distances, the drafting advantage alone is closer to 4 km/h, roughly 13 with DRS"`
- `"3.4 seconds slower than Norris's 2025 pole at the same circuit"`

**Pattern:** Number, then a comparison the reader can feel. Abstract quantities are made visceral.

### Statistical Results Are Narrated, Not Dumped
Stats are woven into prose with plain-English interpretation following immediately:

- `"Rho = 0.65. That's not just 'does the pass work when you try it'- it's 'do cars even show up close enough to try.'"`
- `"L/R was significant at p below 10 to the negative sixteenth"` (no notation like p < 1e-16; written out in words)
- `"DRS activation carries a coefficient of 1.75, increasing the odds of an overtake nearly sixfold"` (coefficient then human meaning)

### The "I Tried Everything" Move
He establishes rigor by listing what he tested before revealing what worked:

- `"I tested everything: straight length, corner radius, track width, speed differential at the braking point, DRS zone length. Every geometric and kinematic variable I could extract..."` then: `"One ratio outperformed them all."`

## 6. How Humor Is Deployed

### Self-Deprecation
- `"I just said it so you'd keep reading."`
- `"a title I do not approve of. I willl leave it as a cautionary tale of letting Claude make plots for you (I'm kidding)."`

### Deadpan Sarcasm (Late in Sentences)
- `"Cool. Great. Exactly what everyone wanted. (Nobody wanted this.)"`
- `"for a governing body that sometimes seems allergic to good decisions"`

### Comic Tricolons
- `"The Theorem Nobody Expected, Wanted, or Cares About"`
- `"human skill, bravery, and the quality of a late-braking lunge"`

### Understatement After Drama
- After a 200-word technical buildup about the physics: `"I mean come on."`
- After describing a fundamental regulatory failure: `"That's not racing. That's a spreadsheet."`

### Parenthetical Punchlines
- `"(Nobody wanted this.)"` - The parenthetical format makes it feel like a muttered aside.

**Key rule:** Humor is always in service of a point. It never derails the argument. It arrives after the technical work is done, as a reward for the reader's attention. The ratio is roughly 85% serious, 15% funny.

## 7. How Charts Are Referenced

### Before the Chart
A brief narrative setup that tells the reader what to look for:

- `"Who also made this plot below, with a title I do not approve of."`
- After explaining the correlation: the chart appears as visual confirmation.

### The Chart Itself
Markdown image syntax with a punchy alt text / title:

```
![The One Ratio That Predicts Every Pass](/images/charts/regression_lr_success.png)
```

Titles are dramatic, not descriptive: `"The Smoking Gun"`, `"The One Ratio That Predicts Every Pass"`, `"The Correlation Barely Moves"`.

### After the Chart (Caption)
Italicized caption that explains what the reader is looking at in plain language:

```
*Each dot is a real F1 corner. Blue = corners without much DRS activity, orange = DRS-heavy zones. The green curve is the trend. Higher L/R, more passes stick.*
```

**Pattern:** Captions use short declarative sentences. They restate the visual in words. They use color references (Blue =, Orange =). They end with the takeaway.

### Narrative After the Chart
The text continues as if the chart just proved the point, often escalating:

- After the first chart: `"And when I switched the y-axis from success rate to raw overtakes per race- the correlation got even stronger."`

## 8. Opening and Closing Patterns

### Article Opening
The F1 article opens with a **provocative claim immediately undercut**:

> `"That's unfair to say, of course. I just said it so you'd keep reading."`

Then pivots to the real thesis via: `"But if you've been paying attention... what I found- well- prettty interesting I thought. So I'm here to share."`

**Pattern:** Hook (dramatic/unfair claim) -> Acknowledgment (self-aware wink) -> Reframe (the real question) -> Promise ("I'm here to share" / "I built a model to find out")

The "Introducing Myself" post opens with a casual direct address: `"Hey, I'm Dustin Landers (if you didn't guess)."`

### Section Openings
Sections often open with a simple scene-setting or a bold restatement:

- `"Here is the simplest overtake in Formula 1. Two cars, one straight, one hairpin. That's it."`
- `"Imagine a dial."`
- `"Okay so the 2026 regulations changed four things simultaneously."`
- `"Let's make up two kinds of passes..."`
- `"The physics of overtaking is not broken. It is being bypassed."`

**Pattern:** Often imperative or invitational ("Imagine", "Let's", "Here is"). Sometimes a blunt thesis statement. Always short. Never more than two sentences before the section gets moving.

### Section Closings / Transitions
Sections end with a bridge sentence that propels the reader forward:

- `"And the 2026 regulations just blew it to pieces."`
- `"To understand what 2026 destroyed, you first need to understand what existed before it."`
- `"And then 2026 threw it all away."`
- `"The diagnosis is clear. The math also points toward a cure."`
- `"The L/R ratio explains the geometry. But geometry alone didn't produce the racing F1 had."`

**Pattern:** A short, emphatic statement (often with "but" or "and") that reframes everything just said and points toward what comes next. These are miniature cliffhangers.

### Article Closing
The final section ("The Fix") is the shortest. It restates the core finding, proposes a solution, and closes with a restatement of the theorem:

> `"The balance point is still out there. It always is- that is what the theorem guarantees."`

Ends on a note of optimism grounded in math. Then an italicized methodology note serves as a footer.

## 9. Tone Markers and Voice Consistency

### First Person
Always "I" - this is personal analysis, not institutional:

- `"I built a model to find out."`
- `"what I found- well- prettty interesting I thought"`
- `"this is the part that made me do a double take"`
- `"this is the part that keeps me up at night"`

### Contractions
Used freely and consistently: `"That's"`, `"it's"`, `"didn't"`, `"doesn't"`, `"you'd"`, `"isn't"`, `"won't"`. The voice never stiffens into formal non-contracted prose except for deliberate emphasis: `"It is being bypassed."` (the "is" carries weight precisely because contractions are the norm).

### Second Person
Used to pull the reader in: `"if you've been paying attention"`, `"What do you think a 50 km/h battery differential does?"`, `"you first need to understand"`.

### Register Shifts
The voice oscillates between:

1. **High register** (technical/poetic): `"a near-perfect equilibrium between straight-line attack and cornering defense, a knife-edge so fine that overtaking came down to human skill"`
2. **Low register** (conversational): `"Just- all of it. Threw the whole kitchen at this thing."`

These shifts happen within the same paragraph and sometimes within the same sentence. The high register establishes credibility; the low register establishes intimacy.

### Sentence Length Variation
Long complex sentences (30+ words) are always followed by short ones (under 10 words). This is the single most important rhythmic device:

- Long: `"For years, the physics of trailing one F1 car behind another produced something bordering on the eerie: a near-perfect equilibrium between straight-line attack and cornering defense, a knife-edge so fine that overtaking came down to human skill, bravery, and the quality of a late-braking lunge."`
- Short: `"That balance isn't a coincidence. It's a theorem."`

### Intensifiers
Specific to this voice: `"truly wild"`, `"reallyyy good"`, `"prettty interesting"`, `"breathtakingly simple"`. He uses intensifiers that feel spoken rather than written.

### The "And-" / "But-" Sentence Starter
Frequently starts sentences with conjunctions, especially to deliver a turn:

- `"And the 2026 regulations just blew it to pieces."`
- `"But the physics had other plans."`
- `"And then 2026 threw it all away."`
- `"And- not to be dramatic- but those are fundamentally different sports."`
- `"But the deepest problem isn't speed differentials or battery depletion."`

## 10. Structural Formulas

### The Numbered-List-in-Prose Device
When listing changes or factors, Dustin uses explicit ordinals embedded in prose rather than bullet points:

> `"First: DRS died."` ... `"Second: Overtake Mode replaced DRS."` ... `"Third: the battery became the car."` ... `"Fourth: thirty percent less downforce."`

Each item gets its own paragraph. Each opens with the ordinal word followed by a colon, then a short declaration, then a full paragraph of explanation.

### The Named-Concept Device
He names concepts with bold text to make them memorable and referable:

- `"**speed**, born from aerodynamics"`
- `"**line**, born from geometry"`
- `"**grip-limit pass**"`
- `"**battery pass**"`
- `"**phantom slipstream**"`

**Pattern:** Bold the term, then define it. Once named, use it freely without bold in subsequent mentions.

### The Real-World Anchor
Abstract findings are immediately grounded in specific real-world examples:

- `"Suzuka's hairpin sits at L/R of 8. Success rate: 22 percent. Baku's Turn 1 sits at L/R of 79- success rate 58 percent"`
- `"Melbourne, March 2026. The season opener at Albert Park..."`
- `"Max Verstappen's battery drained on the formation lap."`

### The Quote-from-Authority Device
Real quotes from real people, attributed with context:

- `'"Formula E on steroids," Verstappen had called the new cars during pre-season testing, and the comparison was not a compliment.'`
- `'"We've come from the best cars ever made in Formula 1 and the nicest to drive," he told reporters, "to probably the worst. It sucks."'`

**Pattern:** Quote is introduced or followed by a one-sentence editorial that tells you how to read it ("and the comparison was not a compliment", "Norris was blunter").

## 11. What NOT to Do

Based on what is conspicuously absent from Dustin's writing:

- **No em dashes.** Ever. Only hyphens.
- **No bullet-point lists** in the body text (numbered items are written as prose paragraphs).
- **No hedging language** like "it seems" or "it appears" or "arguably." He commits to his claims.
- **No academic passive voice.** It's always "I built," "I tested," "I found" - not "a model was constructed."
- **No emoji.**
- **No exclamation marks** (except implicitly through fragments and rhythm).
- **No subheadings within sections.** Each `##` section is a single continuous flow.
- **No footnotes or endnotes.** Methodology goes in an italicized closing paragraph.
- **No "In this article, I will..."** framing. The article just starts.
- **No "In conclusion..."** framing. The final section just delivers the conclusion.
- **No semicolons in casual passages.** Semicolons appear only in technical sentences where two independent clauses present parallel data points.

---

## 12. Quick-Reference Checklist for Writing in This Voice

1. Use hyphens (`-`) as dashes, never em dashes
2. Open with something provocative, then immediately undercut or reframe it
3. Alternate long technical sentences with very short punchy ones
4. Break the fourth wall regularly ("Right?", "I mean come on", "Like just sit with that")
5. Name your concepts in bold and give them memorable labels
6. Ground every number in a comparison the reader can feel
7. Put humor after the hard work, not before
8. Use "First: ... Second: ... Third: ..." for lists, not bullets
9. End each section with a short bridge sentence that points forward
10. Start sentences with "And" and "But" freely
11. Use occasional stretched words ("reallyyy", "prettty") for spoken emphasis
12. Keep headers short, dramatic, and slightly irreverent
13. Write in first person, use contractions, address the reader as "you"
14. Use italicized captions under charts with color-coded explanations
15. Close the article with restated optimism grounded in the core finding
