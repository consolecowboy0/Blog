# Dustin Landers - Style Guide

Reference for matching voice and tone, derived from "Why F1 Overtaking Broke" and "Introducing Myself."

---

## Tone & Voice

- **Conversational authority.** Knows the material cold but talks to you like a friend explaining it over drinks. Never lectures. Never hedges with academic qualifiers.
- **First person, present tense bias.** "I built a model." "I found." "Here is the question that matters." Pulls the reader into the process as it unfolds.
- **Enthusiastic nerd energy.** Genuinely excited by the math and not embarrassed about it. "I mean come on." "Right? Like just sit with that for a second."
- **Controlled irreverence.** Pokes fun at institutions ("a governing body that sometimes seems allergic to good decisions"), at himself ("with a title I do not approve of"), and at the subject matter ("That's not racing. That's a spreadsheet.").

## Sentence Patterns

- **Short punchy declaratives after long analytical builds.** A dense paragraph of physics or data will land on a one-liner: "The balance is real. It's measurable. And the 2026 regulations just blew it to pieces."
- **Fragment sentences for emphasis.** "Poof. Gone." / "Just sitting there. Watching cars go by." / "That's it."
- **Tricolon and list rhythms.** Groups of three are everywhere: "human skill, bravery, and the quality of a late-braking lunge." Often the third item is the longest or most surprising.
- **Conversational interjections mid-paragraph.** "And honestly?" / "Like- completely." / "Cool. Great. Exactly what everyone wanted. (Nobody wanted this.)"
- **Italics for spoken emphasis.** Uses *italics* the way you'd stress a word out loud: "it's *real*", "the car *needs*", "only the trailing car."

## How Technical Concepts Are Introduced

- **Plain language first, jargon second (if at all).** "a flap in the rear wing that, when opened, cut drag" comes before any acronym.
- **Parenthetical definitions.** Technical terms get brief inline glosses: "MGU-K's (Motor Generator Unit-Kinetic, the car's electric motor)."
- **Analogy and metaphor.** "the FIA operated as a regulatory thermostat." "Like Venetian blinds." The analogy does the heavy lifting; the technical detail follows.
- **Numbers made visceral.** Never just states a stat; makes you feel it. "38ms" becomes "less time than a single frame of cinema film sits on the screen."

## Humor Style

- **Dry, deadpan.** The joke lands flat on purpose. Parenthetical asides do a lot of work: "(Nobody wanted this.)"
- **Self-deprecating.** Makes fun of his own enthusiasm and process: "a title I do not approve of. I will leave it as a cautionary tale."
- **Clickbait confession.** Opens with a provocative claim, then immediately admits it: "That's unfair to say, of course. I just said it so you'd keep reading."
- **Exaggerated understatement / overstatement whiplash.** "The Theorem Nobody Expected, Wanted, or Cares About" as a section header. Deflates importance of something he clearly thinks is fascinating.

## Dash Usage

- **Hyphens (-) used as em dashes throughout.** This is a deliberate stylistic choice. Single hyphens serve as em dashes with no spaces: "what I found- well- prettty interesting" / "and the regulation change that shattered it."
- **Frequent use.** Dashes are the primary tool for parenthetical asides, interruptions, and dramatic pauses. They create an informal, thinking-out-loud rhythm.
- **Never uses actual em dashes (---).**  Always a single hyphen doing double duty.

## Section Headers

- **Sentence case, often punchy or slightly wry.** "The Worst Cars Ever Made" / "Two Advantages, One Corner" / "The Fix"
- **Mix of grand and deflating.** "One Number to Rule Them All" (grand) next to "The Theorem Nobody Expected, Wanted, or Cares About" (deflating).
- **Short.** Typically under eight words.
- **Horizontal rules (---) between major sections** to create breathing room.

## Paragraph & Structure Patterns

- **Opens with a hook, then immediately undercuts or contextualizes it.** The lede is designed to grab; the second sentence earns trust by being honest about the grab.
- **Data paragraphs end with interpretation, not numbers.** The stat is never the last word; the meaning is.
- **Sections build toward a crescendo sentence.** Each section has a "thesis line" near the end that crystallizes the point.

## Modulating Toward Warmth (Dad + Kid Piece)

To shift this voice for a piece about a father and his child while staying recognizably Dustin:

- **Keep the conversational interjections but soften them.** "Right? Like just sit with that for a second" becomes reflective rather than argumentative. The wonder shifts from data to the kid.
- **Maintain short punchy sentences for emotional beats** the same way they're used for analytical payoffs. "She just stood there. Looking at it. And I couldn't breathe."
- **Use the same analogy instinct** but draw from domestic life, kid-logic, and small moments rather than physics.
- **Preserve the dry humor** but aim it at himself as a parent. Self-deprecation scales naturally: "a governing body allergic to good decisions" becomes something about his own parenting missteps.
- **Technical concepts become kid-concepts.** The pattern of "plain language first, then the real term" works just as well for explaining how a child sees the world.
- **Dashes still do the emotional heavy lifting.** Mid-sentence interruptions now capture the way a parent's thought gets derailed by feeling: "and then she said- I mean I wasn't ready for this- she said..."
- **Vulnerability lives in the structure, not in flowery language.** The voice stays direct and declarative. Emotion comes from *what* is said plainly, not from adjective pileups. "That's my kid. That's the whole thing."
- **The italics-as-emphasis tool becomes italics-as-awe.** Instead of stressing a technical point, stress the moments of disbelief that parenting produces.
